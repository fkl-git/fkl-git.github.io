Research

Philippine Fiscal Sustainability and Development Trade-Offs

A research project examining how debt service, infrastructure spending, climate vulnerability, and public investment governance shape the Philippines’ long-term development capacity.

Political economy · Public finance · Development governance

Research paper

Central argument

Fiscal sustainability is not only a question of debt levels. It is a question of whether the state can preserve enough fiscal space to finance development, respond to shocks, and sustain public investment without weakening future governance capacity.

Abstract

This research examines the Philippines’ fiscal sustainability challenge through the relationship between debt service, development spending, infrastructure investment, and institutional capacity. Rather than treating fiscal sustainability as a narrow question of whether public debt is manageable, the paper analyzes how fiscal pressure shapes the state’s ability to fund long-term development priorities.

The project argues that fiscal sustainability should be understood as a development question. A government may remain technically solvent while still losing the flexibility needed to invest in infrastructure, climate resilience, social protection, and public services. The central concern is therefore not only whether the Philippines can pay its obligations, but whether it can do so while preserving the fiscal space required for inclusive and resilient development.

Research Question

How do debt service obligations, infrastructure priorities, and institutional constraints affect the Philippines’ fiscal space for long-term development?

Key Findings

01

Fiscal sustainability cannot be assessed only through debt ratios. It must also be evaluated through the state’s ability to protect future development spending.

02

Debt service can create opportunity costs when it competes with infrastructure, education, health, climate adaptation, and other long-term public investments.

03

Infrastructure spending can expand future fiscal capacity when it improves productivity, but it can also narrow fiscal space when projects are poorly selected, delayed, or weakly governed.

04

Climate vulnerability makes fiscal planning more complex because shocks can increase public spending needs while weakening revenue and growth.

Method and Source Base

The paper draws on public fiscal data, government planning documents, audit reports, multilateral assessments, and policy materials related to debt, infrastructure, public investment, and climate-related spending. It combines quantitative fiscal indicators with institutional analysis to examine both the financial and governance dimensions of fiscal sustainability.

Why This Matters

Fiscal sustainability is often presented as a technical problem, but it is also a political and developmental one. The way a state manages debt, spending, and investment determines what it can build, protect, and sustain over time. For a developing economy, fiscal discipline matters not because austerity is inherently desirable, but because public resources must remain available for the future.
